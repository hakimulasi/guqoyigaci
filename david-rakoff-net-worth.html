David Rakoff Overview and Wiki

David Rakoff‘s early life laid the foundation for his multifaceted career as a writer, actor, and essayist. Born on November 27, 1964, in Montreal, Quebec, Canada, Rakoff was the youngest of three children in a Jewish family. His upbringing in a family of intellectuals and his cultural heritage significantly influenced his later work.

The Rakoff family moved from South Africa to Montreal in 1961 for political reasons and then relocated to Toronto in 1967 when David was three years old.

He attended high school at the Forest Hill Collegiate Institute, graduating in 1982. His early education in Toronto played a significant role in his development, both academically and personally.

Early Career in Publishing

Rakoff’s professional journey began in the publishing industry, where he spent 13 years mastering various roles.

He worked as a publishing assistant and a publicist, later moving on to a literary agency and then to HarperCollins as an editor and communications manager. This experience in publishing laid the groundwork for his later success as a writer.

Transition to Writing

At the age of 25, Rakoff started freelance writing while still working in publishing. His talent and dedication eventually led him to become a full-time writer in 1998.

He wrote Q&A interviews for The New York Times Magazine from 1999 to 2002, showcasing his unique perspective and engaging style.

Influence of David Sedaris and Ira Glass

Rakoff’s career took a significant turn when he connected with humorist David Sedaris and producer Ira Glass. After hearing Sedaris on the radio in 1992, Rakoff reached out to him, marking the beginning of a pivotal relationship.

Through Sedaris, he met Ira Glass, who was then a junior reporter at NPR. This connection led to Rakoff’s involvement with This American Life from its inception, where he contributed numerous essays and stories.

Contributions to This American Life

Rakoff became a regular contributor to This American Life, a role that allowed him to explore various themes through personal and often humorous essays. His first piece, Christmas Freud, detailed his experience impersonating Sigmund Freud in a department store window during the holidays.

Rakoff’s contributions to the show were not just limited to writing; he also performed live and even hosted an episode, showcasing his versatility.

Prolific Writing Career

Rakoff’s writing career was marked by his three bestselling collections of essays: Fraud (2001), Don’t Get Too Comfortable (2005), and Half Empty (2010).

Both Fraud and Don’t Get Too Comfortable won Lambda Literary Awards in the Humor category, and Half Empty received the Thurber Prize for American Humor in 2011. His essays were known for their autobiographical and humorous nature, often reflecting on his personal experiences and observations.

Acting and Screenwriting

In addition to writing, Rakoff pursued acting. He appeared in several films and television shows, including As the World Turns and Cosby.

He also acted in theater, notably in plays written by David and Amy Sedaris. Rakoff’s screenwriting talents were recognized with his adaptation of the screenplay for the Academy Award-winning short film The New Tenants.

Voice Work and Directing

Rakoff’s voice work included roles in audio books and animated shows. He voiced Thomas Jefferson in Jon Stewart’s America (The Book) and Leon Czolgosz in Sarah Vowell’s Assassination Vacation. He also directed plays, including works by David and Amy Sedaris, showcasing his skills behind the scenes.
